ABSTRACT
The dissemination of opinions in social networks significantly influences public opinion, mar -
keting, and social governance. The Opinion Maximization (OM) problem aims to select appro -
priate seed nodes to maximize the overall opinion of the network. However, existing research 
primarily focuses on maximizing the number of influenced nodes, and exploration of information 
acceptance and fusion within networks remains insufficient. Therefore, this paper studies opinion 
maximization on social trust networks (STNs) based on an extended continuous opinion dynamic 
model. First, we consider individual stubbornness as a strategic choice and construct a continuous 
opinion dynamic model based on game theory. Then, we propose a two-stage method (T-DQN) 
based on deep reinforcement learning, in which the first stage uses an effective heuristic rule to 
screen potential seeds, and the second stage uses Deep Q-Network (DQN) to determine seeds and 
combines prioritized experience replay and soft update mechanisms to improve learning effi -
ciency. Experiments demonstrate that T-DQN outperforms various baseline methods in maxi -
mizing the sum of final opinions and is robustly applicable to different networks and different 
initial opinion settings (random choice/echo chambers). Additionally, experiments on initial 
cooperation and network structures provide new insights for optimizing opinion guidance and 
information spread.
1. Introduction
With the rapid development of social media, the impact of social networks on individuals has become increasingly evident, and 
relationships between people have become more complex and diverse [ 1 ]. In this context, social network analysis has gradually 
attracted the attention of many scholars. Many opinion dissemination studies [ 2 ] or network optimization studies [ 3 ] focus on un -
directed or unweighted traditional social networks. However, the degree of influence among users in social networks varies. For 
example, ordinary users are more susceptible to the influence of key opinion leaders, while key opinion leaders are almost unaffected 
by ordinary users [ 4 ]. Moreover, the relationship between users often involves opposite types, such as trust/distrust, liking/disliking, 
etc. The trust/distrust relationship and degree between nodes are significant for opinion dissemination [ 5 ]. Therefore, introducing a 
directed weighted signed Social Trust Network (STN) is more realistic [ 6 ].
Influence Maximization (IM) was first proposed by Kempe et al. [ 7 ] and has become a classic problem in social network analysis. It 
has been widely applied in marketing promotion [ 8 ], public opinion governance [ 9 ], and other fields. The diffusion process in IM

research often relies on the Independent Cascade (IC) [ 10 ] and the Linear Threshold (LT) [ 11 ] models. In studies considering only a 
single piece of information, IM aims to find a set of seed nodes in social networks that maximizes the number of influenced nodes 
through information transmission or virus propagation. Gask ´o et al. [ 12 ] used Shapley value approximation as the fitness function to 
identify nodes with the highest contribution to the cascade-influenced set. Zhang et al. [ 13 ] proposed a dynamic GCN-based influence 
maximization algorithm that incorporates a leader false label mechanism, leveraging rich topological information to more effectively 
identify influential seed nodes. With the emergence of competitive information, the problem of Competitive Influence Maximization 
(CIM) has attracted increasing research attention. Competitive propagation models provide the basis for CIM research. For example, 
Pathak et al. [ 14 ] proposed a generalized linear threshold model that incorporates competition to simulate multiple information 
cascades in a network. As a classical application scenario of CIM, Zehmakan et al. [ 15 ] investigated the viral marketing problem with 
competing products and developed a polynomial-time approximation approach that achieves an optimal theoretical guarantee. The 
goal at this point is not only to maximize the number of nodes activated by the target party but also to minimize the number of nodes 
activated by competitors. For example, Dai et al. [ 16 ] defined the objective function of CIM as maximizing the quantitative disparity 
between the expected number of positive and negative nodes that are ultimately activated, taking into account the adverse effects of 
negative opinions.
Opinion Maximization (OM) is a new extension and application of the IM problem. Gionis et al. [ 17 ] introduced the OM problem 
and defined it as selecting a set of k nodes to maximize positive opinions on a given topic. Unlike traditional IM problems, OM in -
tegrates users ’ dynamic opinions, making the problem more practical and meaningful. User opinions will dynamically evolve over time 
[ 18 ]. For instance, if a company aims to enhance its public reputation, an effective approach is to leverage influential individuals to 
disseminate positive opinions through a dynamic opinion model, ultimately maximizing the overall level of positive opinions over 
time.
OM primarily consists of a dynamic opinion model and a seed node selection algorithm. Among them, opinion dynamic models can 
generally be divided into discrete and continuous types. Considering that individual opinions are a continuum of subjective attitudes, 
thoughts, and ideas [ 19 ], this paper focuses on the evolution of continuous opinions. Stubbornness is a common characteristic in 
opinion dissemination, and many studies have argued that stubborn individuals are always stubborn in the process of evolution 
[ 20 , 21 ]. Based on Friedkin-Johnson model, some scholars have regarded individual stubbornness as a predefined characteristic and 
studied the impact of homogeneous and heterogeneous stubbornness on opinion dissemination, enriching research on opinion dy -
namics models [ 22 ]. However, people ’ s dissemination psychology is dynamic and will change with the surrounding environment or 
individual psychological state [ 23 ]. Therefore, based on the classic Friedkin-Johnsen model [ 24 ], this paper introduces game theory 
and constructs an extended continuous opinion dynamic model. This model considers an individual ’ s stubbornness as a strategic 
choice during the evolutionary process, rather than a predefined characteristic. The degree of consensus with surrounding neighbors is 
often a key concern for nodes when updating their opinions, and it is also an important factor influencing individual strategy choices. 
We measure the consensus advantage of individual opinions in a local environment through local interests. In addition, unlike most 
existing studies [ 2 , 25 ], we argue that similar opinions between nodes do not always indicate higher consensus feedback, and it is 
related to the trust relationship between nodes. This paper extends the opinion dynamic model from the perspectives of strategy 
selection and local interests, thereby posing new challenges for OM research.
As another core component of the OM problem, seed node selection has also received considerable attention from researchers. IM 
and OM generally determine seed nodes through greedy or heuristic algorithms. Although greedy algorithms are generally effective, 
they often suffer from high computational complexity. Heuristic algorithms have high efficiency but low accuracy [ 26 ]. To address the 
IM problem, Wang et al. [ 27 ] introduced the MHPD-greedy method, demonstrating that it preserves 98.81 % of the greedy algorithm ’ s 
accuracy with merely 0.11 % of its running time. In addition, deep learning methods are gradually being applied to select seed nodes. 
For example, He et al. [ 28 ] introduced the Q-Learning method when exploring the OM problem of activated opinion dynamic models, 
using a Q table to store the Q-values of discrete states and actions and selecting the optimal action. Later, He et al. [ 29 ] developed a 
multi-stage Q-Learning solution for the competitive opinion maximization problem. While Q-Learning is applicable in settings with 
discrete and compact state spaces, Deep Q-Networks (DQNs) extend this method by employing deep learning to approximate Q values 
in high-dimensional spaces. DQN is particularly well-suited for high-dimensional or continuous state spaces and can handle more 
complex tasks. For example, Ma et al. [ 30 ] introduced an evolutionary deep reinforcement learning algorithm, termed EDRL-IM, which 
formulates the IM problem as a continuous optimization task over the weight parameters of a deep Q-network, combining evolutionary 
algorithms and deep reinforcement learning algorithms to evolve the DQN. Experiments have shown that EDRL-IM outperforms the 
most advanced IM methods in finding seed nodes. To date, the DQN approach has mainly been applied in IM research [ 31 ] but has 
rarely been explored in OM studies.
In summary, current research on OM is limited, with more focus on maximizing the number of nodes influenced. Therefore, we 
extend OM research from two aspects: the opinion dynamic model and the seed selection methods. Considering the dynamic selection 
of individual stubbornness, we propose a game-theoretic opinion dynamics model and develop a two-stage DQN approach to select 
seed nodes from directed weighted signed social networks to achieve OM.
Our Contribution. The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
(1) Propose a new opinion dissemination model from the perspective of strategy selection. Unlike traditional models that treat 
individual stubbornness as a fixed characteristic, we model it as a strategy selection of individuals during the evolutionary process, 
which better aligns with the varying dissemination psychology of individuals in real social contexts.
(2) Develop a two-stage opinion maximization algorithm (T-DQN) based on DQN for continuous state spaces. By restricting the 
action space of the DQN algorithm to five potential seed selection strategies, the complexity of the action space is significantly reduced, 
and the scalability and computational efficiency of the algorithm are improved.

2.3. Two-stage opinion maximization based on DQN
Compared with traditional IM problems, the node opinions in our OM problem are continuous, and the objective function does not 
satisfy the submodularity property. Thus, we introduce deep reinforcement learning methods to enhance the efficiency of seed node 
selection. The traditional Q-learning method suits situations where the state space is small and discrete. DQN employs deep neural 
networks to approximate the Q-value, which is suitable for high-dimensional or continuous state spaces and can handle more complex 
tasks. Therefore, we propose a two-stage opinion maximization method (T-DQN) based on DQN, where potential seeds are selected in 
the first stage and seeds are determined in the second stage (see Fig. 1 ).

3.2. Algorithm Verification
This section compares the five algorithms used as action strategies in the DQN algorithm with our T-DQN algorithm with our T- 
DQN algorithm. We further introduce a Q-Learning baseline that adopts the same candidate seed selection process, reward function, 
and action space as T-DQN, but differs in its state representation by discretizing the cooperation ratio and average opinion into in -
tervals to construct a tabular Q-learning setting. To assess the performance of these algorithms, we vary the number of seed nodes and 
the number of time steps across different networks.
3.2.1. Performance under different numbers of seeds
We set the time step to 30 and demonstrate the performance of seven algorithms on six different networks under different numbers 
of seed nodes, as shown in Fig. 3 . As the quantity of seed nodes grows, the sum of the final opinions gradually increases. In terms of 
baseline action strategies, T-DQN surpasses the five heuristic algorithms, demonstrating the advantage of integrating learning-based 
decisions. Specifically, the MaxDegree and CbC algorithms exhibit the weakest performance across all networks, with highly similar 
results. In contrast, the Blocking algorithm performs well across different networks — except on the Gnutella network, where its 
performance is surpassed by the MixStrategy algorithm. This may be because the Blocking strategy tends to select nodes that act as 
structural bottlenecks or play critical roles in network connectivity, making it more effective in dense or clearly community-structured 
networks. However, in sparse and loosely connected networks like Gnutella, where nodes are scattered and there are few bridging or 
control paths, it becomes difficult for Blocking to identify globally influential nodes. Instead, the MixStrategy algorithm, which 
combines both in-degree and out-degree features, demonstrates greater adaptability in sparse network settings, thereby achieving 
better performance than Blocking on the Gnutella network. In terms of reinforcement learning methods, T-DQN achieves better 
performance than Q-Learning on five of the six networks, which may be attributed to its ability to handle continuous state information 
more effectively, whereas Q-Learning relies on discretizing multiple states, potentially losing important details. Nevertheless, Q- 
Learning achieves better results on the Gnutella network when the number of seed nodes is 40 and 60, and on the BBV and Reddit 
networks Q-Learning ’ s results are close to those of T-DQN. Overall, these results indicate that T-DQN provides a more consistent and 
robust performance across diverse network structures.
It is worth noting that the advantage of the T-DQN algorithm is relatively limited on the BBV network, while it achieves better 
performance on other networks. This may be due to the Blocking algorithm prioritizing nodes with more potential impact objects, 
namely super nodes. In the BBV network, these super nodes already exhibit strong centrality and dominate information flow, reducing 
the optimization space for our seed selection. Moreover, the strategy selection mechanism in the opinion dynamic model further 
amplifies the role of super nodes in propagating opinions, which limits our algorithm ’ s improvement on this network.
3.2.2. Performance under different time steps
We set the number of seed nodes to 40 and demonstrate the performance of seven algorithms on six different networks under 
different time steps, as shown in Fig. 4 . As the time steps increase, the sum of the final opinions first increases significantly, then 
increases slightly, or even decreases. In terms of the performance of different algorithms, T-DQN generally exhibits an obvious 
advantage over the five heuristic algorithms and Q-Learning, although on the Gnutella network with short time steps, CbC and Q- 
Learning achieve slightly better performance. Specifically, the MaxDegree and CbC algorithms still perform relatively similarly and 
have the worst performance on different networks, except on the Gnutella network. In sparse networks such as Gnutella, although T- 
DQN performs slightly worse than the CbC algorithm and Q-Learning when the total time step is short, its advantage becomes more 
apparent as the time step increases. This phenomenon may be attributed to the sparsity of the Gnutella network, which has an average 
degree of only 4.82. The limited connectivity makes it difficult for the influence of seed nodes to reach distant parts of the network 
within a short propagation period. However, when a longer propagation process is allowed, T-DQN can better exploit the network 
structure and optimize seed node selection, thereby achieving more effective opinion spread. In contrast, in denser or more 
community-structured synthetic networks such as BBV, SBM, and WS, T-DQN consistently outperforms other methods under almost all 
propagation step settings. Moreover, in real-world networks such as WikiElec and Reddit, T-DQN also achieves the best opinion 
maximization performance. These findings indicate that our T-DQN algorithm possesses strong generalization and adaptability across 
both synthetic and real-world networks, regardless of whether the network is sparse or dense. This further validates the effectiveness 
and robustness of our T-DQN algorithm.

3.2.3. Performance considering the phenomenon of echo chamber
Considering the common “ echo chamber ” phenomenon in real-world social networks, where individuals within the same com -
munity tend to hold similar initial opinions, we conduct additional experiments on the SBM network with community-consistent initial 
opinions to further validate the effectiveness of our algorithm. We choose SBM instead of BBV, WS, WikiElec, Gnutella, or Reddit 
because these networks present certain limitations regarding community structure. Although BBV and WS are synthetic networks, their 
generation mechanisms primarily emphasize degree distribution or small-world properties, resulting in less distinct community fea -
tures. WikiElec and Reddit, as real-world networks, often exhibit complex or ambiguous community divisions. Gnutella, being a highly 
sparse peer-to-peer network, has scattered connections that hinder the formation of stable communities. In contrast, SBM offers clearly 
defined and controllable community boundaries, making it particularly suitable for simulating opinion propagation under community 
consistency. In this experiment, each community is assigned a randomly generated “ opinion center ” within the range [   1 , 1 ] . The 
initial opinion of each node in the community is then sampled from a Gaussian distribution centered at this opinion center with a 
standard deviation of 0.2, and clipped to remain within the range of [   1 , 1 ] . This setting more realistically captures the convergence of 
opinions within communities and allows us to analyze its impact on overall opinions and algorithm performance.
As shown in the experimental results in Fig. 5 , in terms of overall opinions, the sum of final opinions increases as the number of seed 
nodes grows, but decreases with longer time steps. This downward trend in the sum of final opinions with respect to time steps may be 
attributed to the echo chamber setting, where nodes within the same community start with similar initial opinions. Within a shorter 
propagation duration, opinion dynamics mostly occur within communities. Given that most edge weights are positive, the high opinion 
similarity within communities is likely to result in higher weighted average benefits, making nodes more willing to adopt the opinions 
and strategies of their neighbors and thus achieving a higher final opinion sum. However, as the time steps increase, opinions are more 
likely to spread across community boundaries. Nodes begin to receive conflicting opinions from out- community neighbors, which may 
reduce weighted average benefits and cooperation, ultimately lowering the final opinion sum. This finding suggests that when the echo 
chamber phenomenon is present, a longer opinion evolution process may hinder opinion maximization in social trust networks with 
clearly defined community structures.
In terms of algorithm performance, we observe that our T-DQN algorithm consistently demonstrates superior effectiveness 
compared with the five heuristic algorithms across different numbers of seed nodes and time steps. Although it slightly lags behind the 
Blocking algorithm when the number of seed nodes is small (e.g., 20 seeds), T-DQN steadily outperforms all other heuristic algorithms 
as the number of seeds increases, achieving the best opinion maximization results. Compared with Q-Learning, the advantage of T- 
DQN is somewhat variable when the number of seed nodes changes, but it remains consistently stable across different time steps. This 
outcome confirms that our algorithm remains effective and robust even under varying initial opinion settings.

When there are few seed nodes, most nodes in the network cannot directly or indirectly receive their influence, and a higher initial

pursuit of maximizing opinions, the increase in the number of seed nodes plays a more critical role than the initial cooperation ratio. As 
the quantity of seed nodes grows, their overall influence on the network strengthens, making it easier to guide individual opinions. In 
practice, optimization of seed node selection should not be neglected even if the initial proportion of stubborn nodes is very high; 
otherwise, opportunities to effectively intervene in the evolution of network opinions may be missed.

5. Conclusion
This paper investigates the problem of opinion maximization (OM) in social trust networks, proposes a game-based opinion dy -
namic model, and designs a two-stage seed selection method (T-DQN) based on deep reinforcement learning to optimize the selection 
of seed nodes. Experimental results demonstrate that the T-DQN algorithm outperforms the baseline algorithms in maximizing the sum 
of final opinions and maintains strong robustness across different types of networks. In addition, simulation experiments indicate that:
(1) Under different initial opinion settings, the time steps of opinion dissemination have varying impacts on maximizing opinions. 
In networks exhibiting the echo chamber phenomenon, where nodes within the same community hold more similar initial opinions, 
longer opinion evolution can hinder the achievement of opinion maximization, particularly in social trust networks with well-defined 
community structures.
(2) Compared with the initial cooperation ratio, the number of seed nodes plays a more critical role in maximizing opinions. When 
the number of seed nodes is small, a higher initial cooperation ratio can facilitate the convergence of local opinions. However, as the 
quantity of seed nodes grows, the influence of the initial cooperation ratio on the final opinion distribution diminishes significantly. 
Therefore, when pursuing opinion maximization, even when the initial proportion of stubborn nodes is high, optimizing the selection 
of seed nodes remains crucial to effectively intervene in the evolution of network opinions.
(3) A lower network average degree facilitates opinion maximization. As the network average degree increases, the sum of final 
opinions shows a significant downward trend. Therefore, in certain application scenarios of opinion maximization, such as online

(4) In opinion maximization, adjusting network conductance according to the underlying network structure is critical. For clearly

geometrically embedded and structurally complex networks like HRG, maintaining a medium conductance is most conducive to

Our research enriches the theoretical study of OM problems and provides new ideas and methods for opinion guidance and in -
formation dissemination optimization in social networks. Nevertheless, several limitations remain. First, our model is based on a fixed

further research is needed on its applicability in multi-topic, multi-level, and competitive networks.
